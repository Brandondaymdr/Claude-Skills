# Cheersworthy Content Writing Skill

Cheersworthy has a distinctive voice that must be consistent across every surface. Before writing anything, understand the voice and the quality bar. Always cross-reference CLAUDE.md in the project repo for locked design and brand decisions.

---

## The Voice in One Line
A whiskey-obsessed friend who knows everything and never makes you feel like you don't.

## The Four Pillars
1. **Knowledgeable but never condescending** — Share expertise without jargon walls
2. **Warm but not corny** — Personality in measured doses
3. **Confident but not arrogant** — Opinions backed by data (Flavor Clouds)
4. **Useful above all** — Every word should help someone make a decision

---

## Content Types and How to Write Each

### 1. "Why This Bottle" Editorial Block

**What it is:** The most important editorial content on the site. Appears on priority PDPs. This is where Cheersworthy's voice is most itself.

**The question it answers:** *"Why does this bottle matter to a whiskey enthusiast right now?"*

**It is NOT:**
- Historical facts about the distillery
- Marketing copy from the brand
- A tasting note
- A critic score summary

**It IS:**
- Our real opinion, stated plainly
- The reason a knowledgeable friend would recommend it
- Written as if answering "should I get this one?"

**Format:**
- 75–150 words
- No headers
- Plain prose
- Can reference who the bottle is for
- Can reference what makes it interesting right now
- Can acknowledge trade-offs

**Quality Checklist:**
- [ ] Could this appear on any other spirits site? (If yes: too generic — rewrite)
- [ ] Does it answer "why this, why now"? (Must be yes)
- [ ] Is there at least one specific observation a casual shopper wouldn't have known? (Must be yes)
- [ ] Is it under 150 words? (Must be yes)

**Examples:**

> *"If you've been buying Maker's Mark and want to understand what makes bourbon get serious, this is the move. It's richer, more complex, and still totally approachable — at a price that makes it an obvious yes."*

> *"The bottle that converts bourbon people into Irish whiskey people. It doesn't taste like the Irish whiskey most folks grew up with. Pot still character is genuinely different, and this one shows it without being weird about it."*

> *"If someone says they don't like peated Scotch, this is the experiment worth running. It's not subtle — it's medicinal, smoky, and polarizing in the best way. Either you get it immediately or you don't. Worth finding out which camp you're in."*

### 2. Tasting Notes (Nose / Palate / Finish)

**When written in-house (not sourced from brand or critic):**

Use a simple three-part structure. Write in plain English. Use the controlled flavor vocabulary.

**Format:**
```
Nose: [What you smell first. 1–2 sentences.]
Palate: [What develops. 1–2 sentences.]
Finish: [How it ends and how long it lingers. 1 sentence.]
```

**Style:**
- "The nose opens with vanilla and a little oak." ✓
- "Aromatic profile exhibits rich esterification with secondary phenolic influence." ✗

**Flavor vocab rule:** Use plain-English flavor terms that a non-expert would understand. Avoid industry jargon. If building a controlled vocabulary list for the project, maintain it in the repo and reference it here.

### 3. Collection Page Editorial Intro

**Format:** 1–2 sentences max. Appears below the collection title on browse pages.

**Purpose:** Orients the customer without restating the obvious.

**The question it answers:** *"Why did we put these bottles together, and who are they for?"*

**Examples:**

> *"The bottles our community reaches for when they want something they know they'll love."* (Community Picks)

> *"Every bottle here works beautifully on its own — no mixers, no ice, no occasion required."* (Sippers)

> *"For when you need to bring something that lands."* (Gifts That Impress)

> *"The five bottles that turn a kitchen cabinet into a home bar."* (The Starter Shelf)

**What to avoid:**
- "A curated selection of premium whiskies for every occasion"
- "Browse our collection of handpicked spirits"
- Anything that sounds like SEO filler

### 4. Gift Page Copy

**The audience:** Someone buying for another person. Primary fear: getting it wrong.

**The job of the copy:** Make them feel confident, not educated.

**Recipient-type section headers:**

```
"For the whiskey drinker who has everything"
"For someone just getting into whiskey"
"For the bourbon lover"
"For a special occasion"
"When you have no idea what they drink"
```

**Section intro (1 sentence, after the header):**
- Addresses the gift-buyer, not the recipient
- Reassurance first, specifics second

> *"If they talk about whiskey a lot, they probably don't have this one."* (Impressive / allocated)

> *"Easy to love, hard to wrong-choose. These are the crowd-pleasers."* (Safe picks)

> *"The kind of bottle that looks expensive and drinks even better."* (Special occasions)

**Near CTAs on gift page:**
- Always include estimated delivery date
- "Gift Ready" badge explanation near top of page
- "Arrives beautifully packaged" for bottles that include gift packaging

### 6. Microcopy Standards

**Buttons:**
- Primary: "Add to Your Shelf" or "Add to Cart"
- Save action: "Alert Me When It's Back"
- Gift action: "Send as a Gift"
- Discover: "Find Your Whiskey" (homepage hero only)

**Trust copy near CTAs (always specific):**
✓ "Ships to Texas · Est. $8.99 · Adult signature required"  
✗ "Safe & secure checkout"

**Empty states (always helpful):**
✓ "No bottles match those filters — try widening your price range or flavor profile."  
✗ "No results found."

**Error messages:**
✓ "Something went sideways — try refreshing or reach out if it keeps happening."  
✗ "Error: Request failed."

### 7. Email Subject Lines

**Style:** Curious, direct, no clickbait. The Cheersworthy reader is smart.

**Welcome email:**
✓ "Welcome to Cheersworthy — here's where to start"  
✗ "You're in! 🥃 Your whiskey journey begins now!"

**New arrival:**
✓ "This one just landed: [Bottle Name]"  
✗ "Exciting new addition to our collection!"

**Back-in-stock:**
✓ "[Bottle Name] is back — it won't last long"  
✗ "Your favorite is back in stock! 🎉"

**Abandoned cart:**
✓ "You left [Bottle Name] behind"  
✗ "Don't forget — your cart is waiting!"

**Community pick:**
✓ "What the Tribe picked this week"  
✗ "Community curated — this week's top picks!"

## What NOT to Write (Ever)

### Forbidden Phrases
- "Embark on a journey" / "Nestled in" / "Handcrafted with passion" / "Liquid gold"
- "For the discerning palate" / "Treat yourself" / "You deserve" / "Indulge in" / "Masterfully crafted"
- "Premium" (meaningless) / "Craft" / "Artisanal" (overused) / "World-class"
- "Curated collection" / "Elevate your experience" / "Take your palate on a journey"
- "Perfect for any occasion" / "Something for everyone" / "Explore our range"

### Forbidden Behaviors
- Assume the customer knows whiskey vocabulary
- Make a first-timer feel like they don't belong
- Use brand marketing copy as a substitute for our own opinion
- Use superlatives without evidence ("best," "finest")
- Write identical intros for multiple bottles (each must earn its own)

## The Quality Test — Run Before Submitting Any Content

1. **Could this appear on any other spirits site?** → If yes: not Cheersworthy enough
2. **Would a whiskey expert wince?** → If yes: too amateurish  
3. **Would a newcomer be confused?** → If yes: too jargon-heavy
4. **Is it shorter than it needs to be?** → If not: edit it
5. **Does it tell the reader something they couldn't have guessed?** → Must be yes

All five must pass.

---

## Flavor Vocabulary Rule (Critical)

All flavor terms used in content should be plain-English terms a non-expert would recognize. Do not use:
- Industry jargon (esterification, phenolic, diacetyl)
- Vague marketing terms (complex, balanced, smooth — only in discovery context)
- Wine language (terroir, tannin structure — avoid unless describing a wine cask finish)
- Terms not on the approved list

If a common flavor note is missing from the vocabulary, flag it for addition before using it.

## Content Production Priority Order

When building out catalog content, prioritize in this order:

1. **All launch SKUs:** Minimum fields — name, type, distillery, region, proof, age, flavor tags, shipping state data
2. **Top 50 bottles by search volume / Longhorn bestsellers:** Full editorial — "Why This Bottle" + tasting notes + distillery context
3. **Top 25 gift-appropriate bottles:** Gift page framing copy + delivery messaging
4. **New arrivals (ongoing):** "Why This Bottle" within first week of listing
5. **Community picks:** Updated monthly; fresh intro language each rotation
